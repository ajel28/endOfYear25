Nintendo

Past

Nintendo’s current flagship console is the Nintendo Switch, launched in 2017, with two key follow-ups: the Switch Lite (2019) and Switch OLED (2021). The Switch stands out for its hybrid design—playable both docked on a TV and handheld on the go. It uses a custom Nvidia Tegra X1 chip, which is less powerful than Xbox or PlayStation hardware, but optimized for portability and battery life. Nintendo emphasizes creative, family-friendly experiences with its unique Joy-Con controllers, motion controls, and couch co-op design. It lacks the raw horsepower of its rivals but shines in usability, portability, and charm.

Current

As of 2025, a successor to the Switch is expected soon, but hasn’t officially released. Current pricing puts the Switch OLED at ~$349, the original model at ~$299, and the Switch Lite at ~$199. The platform features best-selling exclusives like The Legend of Zelda: Tears of the Kingdom, Animal Crossing: New Horizons, Super Mario Odyssey, and Super Smash Bros. Ultimate. Online multiplayer is available through Nintendo Switch Online ($19.99/year base or $49.99 with expansion pack), which also offers classic NES, SNES, N64, and GBA games. Unlike PS5 and Xbox, Nintendo games rarely get steep discounts, but they hold their value well and continue to sell long after release.

Pros and Cons

Nintendo’s strengths lie in exclusive first-party titles, portability, and family appeal. The Switch is ideal for casual gamers, kids, and longtime Nintendo fans who enjoy franchises like Mario, Zelda, Metroid, and Pokémon. It’s also great for local multiplayer and indie games. However, limitations include dated hardware, no native 4K support, weaker third-party game performance, and a less robust online infrastructure compared to PlayStation or Xbox. Still, the Switch’s charm, versatility, and iconic game library make it a standout system—especially for players who value gameplay innovation over raw specs.
